The pattern we will discuss is what I call the “3-2-1” Pattern and in this section we will discuss how to apply it to the various “Pick” type games! Those of you who may have the book “The Method for Slot Machines” may recognize this pattern. Although similar, there are some adjustments that were necessary because of the differences in tracking individual results as in playing slots rather than individual numbers which are in the lottery. However, the basic pattern is much the same and reacts in a similar way. At present the “3-2-1” Pattern is the most successful pattern you can play for state lotteries! Other patterns which work very well at other games do not seem to do as well with lotteries.

The basis of the “3-2-1” Pattern when applied to Pick, 3, 4, 5, and 6 games is as follows; an individual number will show very often for 5 to 10 consecutive games in one column, then for the next 5 games it will show only once or not at all, then the last 5 games it will not show at all At this point, after the last five games the odds of this number are greatly increased to show for the next 5 games! In fact, when a number reacts this way on average it can be five times more likely to show than when this pattern is not present! This brings us to our THIRD rule:

For any number to qualify as a “1” in the “3-2-1” Pattern it must not be drawn at all the five consecutive games immediately following the five games in the “2” part of the “3-2-1” Pattern (games sixteen through twenty)!


In the example above the 5 qualifies completely with the “3-2-1” Pattern because it did not show at all the remaining five games. This qualifies all three parts of the “3- 2-1” Pattern! At this point the 5 has a greatly increased chance of showing within the next five games in column one. Since we only have one number that is fully qualified we would use that number in column one for the next five games and combine it with any number/s in the other columns that were drawn twice in the first ten games, and once or not at all in the next five games, and not at all in the last five games! For example, using our master sheet above, which is a complete record of the numbers drawn in the last twenty games, we see that the 4 was drawn twice in the first ten games, only once in the next five games and not at all in the last five games. The 8 and the 9 also qualify according to this rule as well. The zero was drawn twice in the first ten games, but was drawn three times in the last five games which is a violation of the rule.

Going on to the third column we see we have several qualifiers to the rule. The 6 was drawn twice in the first ten games but not at all in the next ten games so it is qualified to play. The zero is also qualified for the same reason. The 9 was drawn twice in the first ten games but also in the last five games so it is not qualified. The 2 is also qualified because it was drawn twice in the first ten games, only once in the next five games and not at all in the last five games. I will list several examples of qualified “3-2-1” Pattern plays using various master sheets, but first I will list the fourth and fifth rules below.


FOURTH rule:

When you have a fully qualified “3-2-1” Pattern number or numbers in all columns, regardless of the number of columns, you will only play those numbers in a combination ticket for the next five consecutive games!


If you have one or more columns in which you do not have a qualified “3-2-1” Pattern number, you will play any number that qualifies according to the following conditions:

A—The number must have been selected twice within the first ten games and...

B—The number must have been selected once or not at all in the five games following the first ten games (games eleven through fifteen) and...

C—The number must not have been selected at all in the last five games (games sixteen through twenty).


These rules are also for Pick-4, Pick-5, and even Pick-6 games because you are tracking only one column at a time in looking for your “3-2-1” Pattern numbers.


FIFTH rule:

If you have any column that has no numbers that qualify for play you then have a choice, you can use all the numbers in that column and combine them with the numbers that do qualify, or you can play any number that has not been drawn at all within the last ten games, and if that does not give you at least one number for the otherwise unqualified column, you can play any number that has not been drawn within the past five games in your combination ticket.

Playing NON-Column Lottery Games:


The “3-2-1” Pattern method can also be applied to lottery games in which the numbers you choose are a series of one and two digit numbers up to as many as fifty two. Above we showed how to play those games in which you selected your numbers in separate columns. Some Pick-5 and even Pick-6 games are like this, especially in other countries. For the most part though in this country any games involving selecting more than four numbers are in a simple “1,2, 3” style format. The typical lottery game is a series of numbers from one to about fifty in which you select five of the numbers. For the Super-Lotto, Powerball, and Cash Ball type games you would also select a single number from another set of numbers in a box below the first group of numbers.


Regardless of the actual set up of the game you are familiar with in your state the procedure for applying the “3-2-1” Pattern method is the same for all these games. Basically we are looking for numbers that have show up more often than other numbers in the first few games, less often in following games, and not at all in the most recent games. The difference in using the method on these games is the frequency of games. Many Pick games are played several times per week so you will have more plays more often. The big games tend to be once or twice per week so it will take longer to accumulate enough information to determine what the live numbers are.


The infrequency of these games will have little effect on our procedure because we will need to use fewer games to base our qualifications on. We will base our records on three game cycles, three of them in consecutive order. The reason for the fewer games has to do with nature of the way the game is played. When dealing with lottery games which are divided into columns we needed a longer game cycle because the possibilities for each number in each column are very limited, but there are several separate columns! When playing other lottery games we look upon the play-board as simply one big column even though the numbers may range from one to over fifty. This means that each individual number has a much stronger reaction in upcoming games when it does show because the odds of it showing in the first place were much higher. Therefore, fewer games would be needed to qualify a single number when that number has a higher mathematical chance of being drawn.


We will begin by using the results of three consecutive games. For now we will explain how to handle the top section of most Lottery games that may involve choosing one number in another area below the area that you must select the four, five, or six numbers. I will show you how to select that one number shortly.

Rule One: First Three Games (The “3”):

Using a blank lottery ticket you will mark all the numbers that were drawn in the first three lottery games. (These first three games would be the “3” in the “32-1” Pattern.) Any numbers that were drawn more than once in those first three games you will circle.


Rule Two: Second Three Games (The “2”):

Any number that was selected ONCE in the first three games (the “3”) CANNOT be selected in the second group of three games at all. If they are drawn again they are to be disregarded and crossed off the list of numbers that were drawn. For those numbers that were selected TWO or more times, they can be drawn once in this second group of three games, but no more than once. If they are drawn more than once they are also to be crossed off and not used.


Rule Three: Third Group of Three Games (The “1”):

Any number that was selected in the first three games and has not been crossed off because of rule two above, must not be drawn even once in the last group of three games (the “1”).


Rule Four:

Those numbers that have passed the qualifying rules above are to be played for the next three games. You will combine them so that all of them are played with the others that have qualified.


To summarize, any number that was selected ONCE in the first three games cannot be drawn again for the next six games. Those numbers that were drawn more than once in the first three games can be drawn once more in the second group of three games, but not at all in the last three games.


If you are playing a game that requires you to select four numbers the most you could have is a total of twelve qualifying numbers. For a five pick game you could have as many as fifteen numbers that are qualified. Most of the time you have less than the maximum because some numbers will be selected two or more times and others will be crossed off because they were selected again in the last two groups of three games.
